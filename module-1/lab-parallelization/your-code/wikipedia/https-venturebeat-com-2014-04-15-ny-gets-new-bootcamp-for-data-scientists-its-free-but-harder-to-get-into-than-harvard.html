NY gets new bootcamp for data scientists: It’s free, but harder to get into than Harvard

\n\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\tEric Blattberg\t\t\t\tApril 15, 2014 9:02 AM\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t
\n\t\t
\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t
Image Credit: The Data Incubator
\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t
\n\n\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t

Finding a great data scientist can feel like searching for\xc2\xa0Princess Peach. She’s always in another castle.

\n

There are plenty of programmers who can match a startup\xe2\x80\x99s pace. There are plenty of PhDs with solid research backgrounds. But there\xe2\x80\x99s a serious dearth of job applicants equipped with both skill sets.

\n

Foursquare veteran Michael Li is working on a solution: a hacker bootcamp for data scientists. It\xe2\x80\x99s called The Data Incubator.

\n

The New York startup intends to take the brightest science and engineering PhDs and propel them into data science careers. The inaugural program is scheduled to begin this June.

\n

It\xe2\x80\x99s not designed for folks starting at square one. Applicants should already have some programming experience as well as strong qualitative and communication skills. Li plans to further familiarize his fellows with the tools and technology stack employers actually care about.

\n

That\xe2\x80\x99s an attractive proposal for PhDs uncertain about their next move. But it gets better.

\n

Beyond room and board expenses in New York City, the six-week program won\xe2\x80\x99t cost participants a penny. Instead of student tuition, The Data Incubator will charge its employer partners if they decide to hire Data Incubator alumni as data scientists or quantitative analysts (quants).

\n

It\xe2\x80\x99s the same model employed by Hacker School, another New York program for coders. More often, however, programming bootcamps will charge for their courses upfront (General Assembly) or take a slice of their alumni\xe2\x80\x99s first-year salaries (App Academy).

\n

Li doesn\xe2\x80\x99t think it\xe2\x80\x99s right to make PhDs pay for technical training after all the other expenses they\xe2\x80\x99ve incurred at university.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe\xe2\x80\x99re really trying to turn the educational model on its head,\xe2\x80\x9d said Li. \xe2\x80\x9cThe idea that you should pay for your own training when you\xe2\x80\x99re so close to being employable — I just don\xe2\x80\x99t think it\xe2\x80\x99s right.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

An autobiographical startup

\n

Li, who is The Data Incubator’s\xc2\xa0executive director, has worked as a plasma researcher at NASA, as a quant at major financial institutions, and most recently as a data scientist at Foursquare. Simultaneously, he worked toward a computational and applied mathematics PhD from Princeton, which he received last year.

\n

But he was frustrated that the skill set he gleaned from his academic studies often didn\xe2\x80\x99t align with professional expectations. In his private sector work, standards rose, and timelines shrank.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cYou go from spending five years of your life working on one really hard, deep problem to having five days to get your project done, if you\xe2\x80\x99re lucky,\xe2\x80\x9d he said.

\n

While academics get their jobs by delving deep into the details, most companies have little tolerance for minutiae. They\xe2\x80\x99re looking for the quick and dirty solution, not flawless code with every little detail ironed out.

\n

As the scale of data accessible to us grows, that skill gap is becoming even more acute, Yann LeCun, Facebook\xe2\x80\x99s director of artificial intelligence research, told VentureBeat. LeCun, who is also the founding director of New York University\xe2\x80\x99s Center for Data Science, says the educational system has yet to catch up.

\n

Although Li was clearly a stellar student (a former math professor attested to his academic aptitude), he felt plenty of pain points both in getting hired and in his first few months on the job.

\n

But now, as Li marches toward The Data Incubator\xe2\x80\x99s June launch, those troubles have become one of his greatest assets: He knows what knowledge gaps others need to fill. And he knows how to fill them.

\n

Teaching brilliance

\n

In designing The Data Incubator\xe2\x80\x99s curriculum, Li took a cue from art schools.

\n

Throughout the six-week program, each fellow will craft a portfolio project. It should showcase their ability to gather and clean data, apply some meaningful machine learning methods and statistical analyses, and showcase or visualize the results in an engaging, digestible manner. By the end of the program, they\xe2\x80\x99ll have a substantial piece of code for potential employers to review.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI have some modules written for various things that they should know, but it’s mostly me guiding them through building a portfolio project,\xe2\x80\x9d said Li.

\n
\n

The curriculum

\n

Software engineering and numerical computation. Numerical techniques for optimization and vectorized linear algebra. Programming tools including Python, NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib. Data visualization including d3, ggplot.
\nNatural language processing. Handling unstructured data, stemming, bag of words, TF/IDF, topic modeling.
\nStatistics. Hypothesis testing, regression and classification, ensemble methods, cross-validation, variance-bias decomposition, data normalization.
\nDatabases and parallelization. SQL, Hadoop, MapReduce, Hive.

\n
\n

Along the way, fellows will familiarize themselves with key technical skills, spanning software engineering and numerical computation to databases and parallelization. They won\xe2\x80\x99t all come away with the same skill set, but there will be some common ground, like a focus on Python.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf you want to be a data scientist, these are the things\xc2\xa0you\xc2\xa0need to know,\xe2\x80\x9d said Li.

\n

We lucky few

\n

Li is currently sorting through more than 1,000 applications.

\n

Together, Data Incubator applicants represent over 80 different universities. They\xe2\x80\x99re mainly PhDs and post-docs, but some junior faculty and assistant professors have also applied to Li\xe2\x80\x99s bootcamp.

\n

Li has yet to decide how many students will make it into the first batch, but he assured us it\xe2\x80\x99ll be a small number.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe cannot accept 5.8 percent,\xe2\x80\x9d he said, referencing Harvard\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance rate. \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s just not possible.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

That low total will enable Li to pick the folks with the intellectual firepower to excel in the world of data science. They\xe2\x80\x99ll come pre-equipped with 90 percent of the difficult-to-learn skills: the math and stats expertise. Li\xe2\x80\x99s program is all about reinforcing that last 10 percent: the technical training, plus some communication and networking skills.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s not like any of [that material] is conceptually complicated: Bright people can pick it up pretty quickly,\xe2\x80\x9d said Facebook\xe2\x80\x99s LeCun. \xe2\x80\x9cThe stuff you can\xe2\x80\x99t pick up on the fly is the math, and these people [already] have it.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

?????, Profit

\n

With some initial financing from a Cornell Tech accelerator program on-hand, Li\xe2\x80\x99s primary focus now is courting the employer partners that will fund his venture in the long-term.

\n

So far, around 20 companies have agreed to participate as employers of the bootcamp’s alumni. Many are in the \xe2\x80\x9cmainstream\xe2\x80\x9d tech sector, including Foursquare and Etsy. Others represent the health care and financial services industries, like oncology data firm Flatiron Health and algorithmic execution broker Quantitative Brokers. Mashable is holding down the fort as The Data Incubator\xe2\x80\x99s first media employer.

\n

Companies don\xe2\x80\x99t have an obligation to hire Data Incubator alumni, but if they do make a successful hire, they\xe2\x80\x99ll pay the equivalent of a recruiter fee.

\n

Normally, when companies post a data science job listing — say, on a university job board — hundreds of resumes come flooding in. It\xe2\x80\x99s difficult for the firm to separate the qualified applicants from the rest of the crowd.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn general, when you look at a resume and interview a candidate, this ends up being a relatively shallow assessment of their ability to study and build new things,\xe2\x80\x9d said Robyn Peterson, Mashable\xe2\x80\x99s chief technology officer.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cLi’s had to face this problem directly, and any way we can help him and some aspiring data scientist do both, we will.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Effectively, The Data Incubator will pre-screen candidates. Plus, the alumni will all have portfolio projects, so employers can dig into some tangible, recent material before running their own tests.

\n

With recent university grads\xc2\xa0often lacking the right skills for the job, companies are showing a preference for more seasoned candidates. But there are a few advantages to hiring straight from a university: Foreign students can get a visa more easily, and folks emerging from degree programs might start with more subject-specific knowledge.

\n

But because there\xe2\x80\x99s no obligation to hire, there\xe2\x80\x99s very little downside to participating in Li\xe2\x80\x99s program.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe\xe2\x80\x99re open to all kinds of ways to get good people,\xe2\x80\x9d said Quantitative Brokers cofounder Robert Almgren. \xe2\x80\x9cWe only really need one guy who is fired up about financial markets and has the skills to add something.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Big data, fewer scientists

\n

San Francisco-based Zipfian Academy, another data science bootcamp, opened its doors last September. General Assembly offers an introductory data science course in New York. Insight Data Science\xc2\xa0runs data science fellowships for post-docs in both Silicon Valley and New York.

\n

So The Data Incubator is not a wholly original idea. But that doesn’t matter.

\n

The data science education market is far from overcrowded: Demand for data scientists continues to outstrip supply. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates that by 2018, the U.S. will face a shortage of 140,000 to 190,000 people equipped with the deep analytical skills necessary to make sense of big data.

\n

That\xe2\x80\x99s why these bootcamps — and other data science education initiatives, from online courses to university programs — are so crucial. If The Data Incubator encourages just one physicist, engineer, or statistician to pursue data science over day trading, that\xe2\x80\x99s a small but not insignificant step toward closing the talent gap\xc2\xa0in this young, amorphous industry.

\n

Li has big ambitions for his little program. He knows a brick-and-mortar bootcamp can only reach a limited audience. In the long run, he hopes to take The Data Incubator online so it can reach thousands.

\n

For now, though, he\xe2\x80\x99s focused squarely on opening the doors this June.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI really want us to be the company that sets the standard for what it means to be a data scientist,” said Li.
